**2. Long Walks Are Therapy**

One morning on campus, I stopped in front of a fallen flower.

I do this sometimes. Just crouch down. Get close. Take a photo.

I noticed something. Then I noticed it again on the next flower. And the next.

Five petals. Every single one.

I Googled it standing there on the path, shoes wet from the dew. Five is apparently nature's number. Some evolutionary answer to a question nobody asked out loud.

I stood there for a long time.

Back home in Mumbai, there's a forest called Aarey. On weekends I'd walk in for about three hours. Just me. Sometimes earphones, sometimes nothing.

Big trees. Farms. Birds I didn't know the names of, their calls hanging in the clean air. I'd stop and photograph them. Lakes with still water. Flowers growing sideways out of rocks.

I wasn't looking for anything in particular.

That's the point.

I have a buddy who walks with me sometimes. Jolly guy. Talks the entire time.

I don't mind. He fills the silence and I watch everything else.

But when I go alone, something happens.

A math problem I'd been stuck on for two days will just: open up. Not because I sat and thought harder. Because I stopped thinking and started walking.

I watched a Richard Feynman interview once. The interviewer asks him how he gets his ideas.

Feynman gets excited. Starts explaining. Describes exactly what he was doing when a particular idea arrived.

From 33:25 onwards the interviewer asks him this [question](https://youtu.be/GNhlNSLQAFE?si=FF4ad5N-FPMQgC4N)

He was walking on the beach.

He didn't say the walk was the point. He was already past it, onto the physics. But I caught it. Maybe because I already suspected it.

I don't call it a theory. It's a hypothesis.

Walking moves something loose.

Whatever's been stuck in there: a problem, a question, something pressing on you quietly, it finds room to shift.

The idea doesn't come at the desk.

It comes when you've stopped demanding it.

I still take photos on walks. Flowers, mostly. Trees sometimes. A bird if it holds still long enough.

Most of the flowers have five petals.

The pattern was always there. I just had to be walking to see it.

**3. Beauty and The Beast**

I've never been in a relationship.

Not once.

When I was in school I was busy with other things. Cricket. Football. Gymnastics. Picking fights with boys from other sections just because It was fun.

Girls weren't even on the radar.

By 10th standard, society's pressure had gotten to me. I wanted to score well. And I did. 88.20%. First time I'd ever crossed 65%.

So I took science and buried myself in physics, chemistry, mathematics.

Then engineering.

Then a Master's degree.

Always focused. Always laser-focused.

Girls? Last thing on my mind.

Part of it was the focus.

But the other part.

The harder part.

I started losing my hair at fifteen.

You don't understand what that does to you unless it happens.

Fifteen years old. Looking in the mirror. Watching your hairline retreat like it's running from something.

It hurted me.

I felt deeply insecure. Couldn't shake it. Wouldn't shake it. Carried it with me everywhere like a backpack full of rocks I refused to put down.

Eventually I accepted it.

Didn't have much of a choice.

And with that acceptance came a quiet assumption. A rule I made for myself without realizing I'd made it.

Approaching a girl was simply not something I would do.

Second semester of my Master's degree.

I noticed her in the mess.

Quiet. Calm. Entirely in her own world.

I was regular about meal times. So was she.

I was the kind of person who didn't like eating alone. I didn't have many friends but I'd gotten used to it.

Seeing her alone though. That made me feel something.

Maybe she didn't like eating alone either.

Maybe I could join her.

I never went up to her.

Worried about giving the wrong impression. Didn't even know her name.

So I let the idea go.

Semesters passed.

I would notice her occasionally. Feel that vague sympathy. Then go back to my academics, projects, IEEE events in Bangalore, career planning.

But the more I saw her around campus, the more something stirred.

I would catch myself wondering.

Should I say hi?

Maybe just say hi?

Then my insecurity would shoot that thought down before it could take flight.

By fourth semester I was more focused than ever on studies and career.

But whenever I saw her in the mess, it hit me.

I was falling for her.

I tried to approach her two or three times.

Each time I failed completely.

I would get so nervous my body would lock up. I feared I would stammer. I've had a stammer problem since childhood. And the baldness was always there in the back of my mind.

Reminding me.

You look far below average.

When I was a kid I used to watch cartoons with my sister.

Every Barbie film. Every Cinderella. Things I never would have chosen on my own.

One of those was Beauty and the Beast.

The Beast is deeply insecure about his appearance. It's the core of who he is.

I saw myself in him.

The insecurities. The stammer. The baldness. All of it collapsing into a low confidence I couldn't shake.

And she was like Belle.

The most beautiful, gentle person I had wanted to be with.

Every time I thought of going up to her, I also thought of how the Beast would look at Belle and then at his own reflection.

Feeling unworthy.

Feeling like he had no right to even stand near her.

But then, in the story, Belle sees through all of it. She sees his gentleness, his vulnerability, and she loves him for who he is. Not how he looks.

It's a beautiful story.

A rare one in the real world.

I had always believed my insecurities would keep me from approaching anyone.

But this time, for the first time, I thought.

Maybe I should just try.

The chances are 99% against me.

But let me go for that 1%.

So I started using a face wash. First time in my life. Just like the Beast preparing himself for dinner with Belle.

I asked a friend which one to use.

I started grooming myself. Something I hadn't bothered with in years.

There was an exhibition on campus.

I saw her there. Perfect opportunity.

I went to the Mac lab where I usually worked on projects. But that day I spent three hours straight brainstorming with ChatGPT about what to say to her.

Called up a childhood friend for advice.

By the time the exhibition was about to close, I finally went.

I stood in front of the first artwork.

Heart hammering.

Hands trembling.

Mind screaming at me to just go back.

Then she appeared out of nowhere.

Right in front of me.

I froze.

I wanted to speak.

But what do you say to someone whose name you don't even know?

She walked away before I could find the words.

Later she came back to collect the artworks as the exhibition was closing.

I thought. This isn't the right moment. I'm too nervous. I'll make a mess of it.

I decided to leave.

But just before walking out, a painting caught my eye (Later I realized, it was her artwork).

It reminded me of my native place, Ratnagiri. The children in it looked just like the ones I used to see there.

I went through the other artworks one by one.

The last one I stopped at was hers.

When I realized it was her work, I just stood there. Staring. Trying to figure out what she was trying to say with it.

Somewhere along the way I got completely lost in it.

When I finally turned around, there was no one left.

Just another girl collecting the last artwork. The one I had been standing in front of.

That was the day I finally learned her name.

I couldn't stop thinking about her that night.

I searched for her on Instagram but couldn't find her.

I think that was the day my feelings became real.

One weekend in the library I came across some art magazines. On a whim I flipped through them to see if her work was there.

My intuition was right.

Not only were some of her exhibition pieces inside, but also works I hadn't seen before.

After the exhibition, knowing her name from her portrait, I started thinking about how to make a good impression when I finally spoke to her.

I was working on a chatbot project at the time.

What if I gave the chatbot her name?

Not just that. The chatbot would have an artistic personality. Crafting responses as if you were talking to someone joyful and deeply in love with teaching.

I would show it to her. She'd be impressed. Something special would begin.

I know how childish that sounds.

I don't watch romantic films. I had no idea how to impress a girl. What to say. How to say it.

But I thought it would work.

I brainstormed with ChatGPT again.

Tried the ideas it gave me.

Failed every single time.

Beautiful women just scare me. I was scared of her. Nervous around her. Terrified of stammering.

And I had never done this before. The weight of that had its own kind of pressure.

Whenever I tried to walk up to her, my mind would abandon me.

It felt exactly like that.

Like my brain would say, "You're on your own, mate," and vanish.

Leaving me standing there like an empty shell.

Eventually ChatGPT gave me advice that actually made sense.

Just be yourself.

Every time I went in with a script, I fell apart.

So the new plan was simple.

Go up to her. Say hi. Tell her I liked her artwork. Let things flow from there.

I rehearsed the opening more than a hundred times that day.

But when I finally walked up to her on the stairs and looked her in the eyes, I felt my brain start drifting again.

I managed the first few words on track.

Then the drift became a freefall.

I went on autopilot. Recited what I had rehearsed.

Then she spoke.

And I heard her voice for the first time.

I went somewhere else entirely.

I introduced myself.

Reached out my hand for a handshake.

She took it.

Our hands met.

I felt the blood rush through me, a current from head to toe.

It's strange how someone's small touch can shake the whole world inside you.

Somehow I kept talking. I don't even know where the words were coming from.

When she spoke a little more, I relaxed slightly. My mind was respawning.

We walked and talked mostly about her artwork. All the way to the path near her hostel.

I didn't want it to end.

We said goodbye.

And that was it.

My first real interaction with a girl.

After all that effort and all that fear.

I went back to my apartment and thought about her all night.

She is cute. Quiet. Polite.

She is exceptional at her craft and genuinely dedicated to her passion. Which is rare.

She is a risk-taker. Following your passion when it's full of struggle takes real courage. I admired that.

I had also wanted to be an artist once.

I remembered how in 5th standard I had to change schools. Terrified of being the new kid with no friends. I had planned that on the first day I would show my partner my art book. Maybe he'd share it with others.

It worked.

Our teacher saw it. Praised me in front of the whole class. Made everyone clap for me.

I made friends on my very first day.

And art quietly slipped away.

I wanted to be her co-pilot. Someone who works on her dreams alongside her with equal dedication and passion.

I thought about all of this well into the night.

Second interaction.

I saw her in the mess. Sitting alone again.

I felt the familiar nervousness. Talked myself through it. Went up to her.

Asked if I could join her.

Started asking about her artworks again.

I was excited.

Her smile is so contagious and warm. Somehow I found myself talking without having prepared anything, and without stammering. Even in English.

After a while I showed her one of my own drawings. An old portrait of Nikola Tesla.

Then she showed me something.

Something I wasn't expecting.

She showed me a portrait she had made.

Of her boyfriend.

My heart broke quietly.

I didn't know what to say.

I kept talking somehow. But inside everything had collapsed.

I told myself.

All is well. You did great. You tried. It didn't work out. But that's okay. Focus on your career.

And that was that.

I sighed. After dinner I walked with her toward her hostel. We said goodnight and goodbye.

And I walked back alone.

I still remember that walk.

From her hostel to my apartment.

Feeling so heavy.

I came to my room. Sat with it. Thought about how hard life can be sometimes.

For the very first time I had approached someone. Pushing past every insecurity. My hair. My stammer. My awkwardness. My English.

And in some ways it had gone well.

But I had already lost her before I'd even begun.

I admired her artworks.

I admire for the kind of person she is.

I like being around people who are passionate and driven.

And I liked her company genuinely.

The next day at dinner I saw her again. Went up. Said hi. Joined her.

But after a while I started to feel like an unwanted guest.

She was on her phone. I was eating. I didn't know what to do.

Then her friend joined. The three of us sat there in silence. No one said a word.

I felt like I was being pushy. Forcing my presence on them with my awkwardness.

I was embarrassed.

I decided to stay behind after dinner instead of walking out with them.

I said goodbye. They went.

I thought that was the right call.

That day I kept worrying I had come across as arrogant.

I didn't mean to.

I just didn't know what to do.

The next day I tried to talk to her again about the previous interaction. To clear the air.

But I may have been too pushy again. Bothering her while she was on a call.

I told myself.

Stop being an unwanted guest.

Stop going back.

I had an umbrella.

Without thinking, without asking, I pulled her under it.

I realized immediately what I'd done. That I was being pushy again.

I said sorry. Walked her to her hostel.

My actions must have made her uncomfortable. I didn't want that. I felt ashamed.

For the next three days I felt low.

And I decided I should stop going up to her.

When I'm around her, my mind leaves me. My insecurities take over. I make things awkward.

Staying away felt like the right thing to do.

On the last day, we were walking toward each other.

I looked at her.

I smiled.

I wanted to say goodbye.

I noticed she looked like she was about to say something too.

But she didn't.

Neither did I.

We just passed each other.

I regretted it immediately.

I had wanted to say goodbye. To say sorry for making things awkward. To tell her I hadn't meant to come across the way I did.

But I said nothing.

It was my last day at college.

I came home to my hometown.

Whenever I thought about those college days, those interactions would come back to me.

And I would regret that last silent passing.

The chatbot project. The one I had built with her personality woven into it.

To meet her again. To say sorry. To wish her well. To tell her I genuinely liked her company and that her artworks were the best I had seen.

My mind abandoned me again.

Meeting her after so long, I panicked and asked for her Instagram and number out of nowhere.

Which must have made her uncomfortable.

I just kept messing up every interaction with her.

The story of the first time I ever approached a girl.

But I wasn't.

I wish her every success.

The Beast didn't get Belle in my version of the story.

For the first time in his life.

He tried.

**5. 8x8 Battleground**

The screen shows I've lost my queen.

Two rooks gone. Twenty moves in.

Any reasonable person closes the tab.

I don't.

I've been playing chess like this for years, no opening theory, no memorized codes, no prepared lines. I don't know what bf4 means. I don't know the name of my first three moves.

I'm not interested.

What I'm interested in is the board in front of me, right now.

Chess players study. They build strategy: piles of pre-analyzed positions, opponent tendencies, openings with names from the 1800s. They arrive to the game already loaded. Move X happens, they play Y. They've planned it. They know where this goes.

I understand why. If you want to win, strategy is the answer.

But I play for something else.

I play for the moment my blood starts moving.

The position where I'm down everything that matters and there's no logical path out, that's the position I want. Because somewhere in that tangle of bad options, there's usually one move nobody sees. One move that doesn't look like survival. One move that, if you're paying close enough attention, turns the whole thing over.

I found one once. Two moves from checkmate. The opponent probably already knew it. I thought I was done.

Then I saw it.

One check. The king had to move. And on the next move, he was done.

I still have the screenshot.

That's what tactics is. Not a Strategy. A read. Eyes on the board, fully in it, nothing else existing for those few seconds while you find what no one else is finding.

Strategy takes the surprise away. That's its whole point.

Tactics lives in the surprise.

**7. Don't Get Used to the Broken**

The cupboard door opens.

Clothes on the left. Books in the middle. Watches, perfumes, documents each in their section.

Something settles in me when I see that.

I show up to things before they start. Not just on time. Before time. I want to walk into a place while it's still empty. See the room. Find where I want to be. Let my body get comfortable before anything asks something of me.

It's the same instinct.

There was a door handle in the house that rattled every time someone touched it. Loose screws. I got a screwdriver one afternoon. Thirty seconds. Done.

A table that clinked when I worked at it. Uneven legs. A folded piece of cardboard under the short one. Quiet.

A leaking tap. Adjust the nut.

Shoes get dirty. Clean them. The fan collects grey dust at the corners. Hand broom. Old tires wear down. Change them before a road you don't know decides to test them.

None of this is dramatic.

But it's easy to stop noticing. The rattling handle becomes background noise. The drip becomes just how the tap sounds. The clink becomes the table.

And then you're living in a slow kind of broken.

A broken heart is the same. It can be stitched. With time and care. But someone has to notice it's still bleeding.

Every morning the cupboard opens.

Clothes on the left. Books in the middle. Watches and perfumes on the right.

It's a small thing.

But that's how you stay ahead of the broken.

**8. Find Your Own Path Or Follow A Prescription**

You're trying to get better at something.

You look for the exact method. You research the right approach. You want certainty before you begin. You want to know it'll work.

Or you just start. You try something. It fails. You adjust. You try again. Slowly, over many attempts, you build a feel for it.

The first one is the chemist. The second is the cook.

A chemist's tools measure down to the smallest unit. The prescription/instructions is followed exactly. If something is off by even a little, the whole thing fails. The chemist waits for the theory before moving.

The cook works differently. Once they've practiced a few recipes, they start making their own. They add more spice if it's needed. How much? A little more. They taste. They know. They use trial and error where the chemist waits for proof.

There are times you'll want to be a chemist. And times you'll want to be a cook.

More often than not, you'll want to be the cook.

Take someone who's never sat on a cycle.

He wants to race at the Olympics.

First, he learns to balance. Then to pedal. Then to shift without looking down. He follows the prescription exactly. He measures his power output. He does what the coaches tell him.

That's the chemist.

Then the training manual runs out.

No coach can write the plan that gets him to the podium. No prescription covers what happens in kilometre 35 when his legs stop working and someone is closing on his left. He has to find that. He has to go somewhere the instructions don't reach.

That's the cook.

Everything that makes someone great happens past the last instruction.

Lionel Messi stayed after training ended. Every day. His coaches didn't ask him to. No prescription sheet covered those hours. He is 5'6" in a sport that favoured taller men. He found what his body could do that theirs couldn't. The low centre of gravity. The burst through the smallest gap. The left foot, always the left foot. He didn't follow a prescription to greatness. He found a path that only his body could walk.

The chemist in you will say: history hides its failures. For every Messi, a thousand players trained just as hard and got nowhere. True. But the cook doesn't need history's permission to try. A failed dish is not a wasted one. It's information. You find out what doesn't work. You adjust. You go again. The chemist wants every attempt to succeed. The cook just wants every attempt to teach.

Particles collided for billions of years before planets formed. You don't have that long. Prescriptions are someone else's chaos, compressed into a shortcut.

Muhammad Ali is my favourite person who ever lived.

I wish I could talk like him.

They drafted him into a war he said wasn't his. He refused. They stripped his title. They took three years of his prime. He went to jail for it. And when he came back, he was still Ali. He stood at stages and said what no one else would. He fought for his people and for peace. He fought every institution that told him to stay quiet. **16. Human recognises human**

You've tweaked your back and now you can't work out.

You've got a deadline and every time someone asks you to hang out, you're too tired to say yes.

One of your tortoise is sick, so going out on a trip is off the table.

Your bike breaks down. You miss the meeting you were banking on.

The approval arrives two weeks late and pushes everything back a month.

The extra part arrives broken. You order another one.

Cooking whole food takes 45 minutes. The packaged stuff takes 10.

There's always going to be something.

The danger is when the something becomes the identity.

An excuse is not dangerous until it turns absolute.

Stack enough of them and the thing you actually wanted fades into the background.

Got a tweaked back?

Good. More time to figure out recovery options and build a plan.

Short on time to cook when you're trying to eat better?

Good. Skip the meal. Let yourself be hungry for a bit. Plan the next few days.

The extra part arrives broken?

Good. Find a workaround. Work on something else while you wait.

Not every block is permanent.

Most of them move.

You clear them with effort, or you clear them with how you think about them.

Once something becomes permanent, it's no longer an excuse. It's just true.

Having kids/pets means they'll always be there. That's not an obstacle anymore. That's you now.

Forgetting something you promised is already done. You can't unforget it.

But you can say: That's my bad. I'm sorry. Let me fix it.

You pour yourself into the art piece knowing there's no finish line. It'll be finished when it's finished.

You spend time with your sick friend knowing no relationship is ever complete. So you show up, patient and present. One day you'll miss the small inconveniences of today.

You call your friend while cleaning the kitchen after three days of two sick days and no chores done.

Think about the last time someone got something wrong. A company, a person, a restaurant.

No one's perfect.

But think about when they followed up. When they corrected it, graciously.

That experience often lands better than if it had been right the first time.

Why?

Because human recognises human.

We know our own flaws. We live inside them.

So when someone else owns theirs, really owns them, we feel it.

There's always going to be something.

An error. A mistake. A roadblock. A forgotten promise.

You can let it sit there.

Or you can pick it up, fix it, and hand it back.

**17. Learning has been Unorthodox**

The teacher writes the formula on the board.

Everyone copies it down.

I sit there with ten questions that no one has asked.

That was 9th grade geometry. The teacher would solve a problem, name the formula, and the class would apply it. Keywords matched to answers. It worked for most people.

Not for me.

I didn't know what the volume of a cube actually meant. I didn't know why pi was a constant but also somehow never fully defined. I didn't know what problem the mathematician was solving when he discovered it. These weren't side questions. They were the main thing. And class was moving on without them.

So I'd come home. Pull out the textbook. Read the introduction. Read the history of the formula. Find the derivation. Work through it slowly, line by line, until something clicked.

The joy of that moment. It was real.

This was a long path. A slower path.

But it was mine.

Vectors came next.

The teacher said force is a vector. Vectors have magnitude and direction.

How does a force have a direction altogether?

What is a unit vector and why does it exist?

I sat through a three-hour science lecture and left with more questions than I arrived with. That used to bother me. Everyone else seemed fine. I had a list.

But I went home. Spent hours with it. Read the history. Built the picture slowly. And a few weeks later I was solving the word problems the professor called difficult, because I could see the vectors first. Place them in space. Orient them. Then choose the formula.

I wasn't faster than my classmates.

But I could see what I was calculating.

Calculus was another planet.

I couldn't understand differentiation without first understanding limits. Couldn't understand limits without seeing the graphs of different functions. So I worked backwards, one layer at a time, until I found the floor.

Then I learned that calculus is used in kinematics. Kinematics was supposed to be taught later. I studied it anyway.

That's the part I actually liked about science. You understand something fully and you start seeing it everywhere.

Around that time I watched a film about [Ramanujan](https://youtu.be/cZSDSKC9z54?si=ZOggD9-KPjnVBOyz) . It changed something. Mathematics stopped being a subject to pass and became a thing people had given their lives to.

I still have the notes I made during those years. Those are precious to me.

After 12th, I watched a film about [Nikola Tesla](https://youtu.be/ecni9SjkWoM?si=bvM-FyxZOErMNpz6) . That was enough. I chose electrical engineering.

I loved the chapter on current electricity. How electrons flow. What forces them through a conductor. The whole thing read like a story once you slowed down enough.

Engineering was more practical, more applied. Less discovery. And then COVID arrived and took the practicals away entirely. My BTech was quieter than it should have been.

In 2023 I joined a master's in data science. Statistics, probability, programming, I had almost none of it. Most classmates had studied computer science or statistics for years. I had come from electrical engineering. Power systems. AC machines. Control theory.

I felt the gap.

But I'd felt that before.

I read an article that year. Peter Norvig's [*Teach Yourself Programming in Ten Years*](https://norvig.com/21-days.html) . One paragraph in and I stopped trying to catch up fast.

I went slow. Again.

The statistics book that helped most was the one taught in baby steps, [Probability Course](https://www.probabilitycourse.com/) with theory written like it was meant to be understood. I kept returning to it whenever I hit something I couldn't see through.

By the end of the first year something shifted. The fundamentals had settled. New concepts started chaining to old ones without much effort. Machine learning made sense once the statistics did. Neural networks made sense once I remembered my calculus. Transformers came later, but by then there was something solid underneath.

I never found the shortcut method.

I couldn't memorize the formula first and ask questions later. Something about that felt like being handed someone else's answer.

I needed to understand the reason before I could use the result.

The long way. The slower way.

But somewhere at home, sitting with a textbook after a lecture I couldn't follow, I found the thing most people were moving too fast to look for.

I still have those notes.

Running from yourself.

Like.

I love you. I love you. I love you.

Line up the story in your head with the one you're living.

Things go sideways when you start playing hide and seek with yourself.

Trying to fit into someone else's shape. Someone else's definition of a life well lived.

Which is silly.

Because no definition of success beats walking into a quiet room and being glad you're there.

If not you, who?

Not yet.

So use the tools. Use them well.

But keep the muscle warm.

Agency is like any muscle.

Unused, it softens.

The more you hand over, the less of it stays yours.

**22. He Just Painted**

There's a word for it. Imposter syndrome. The feeling that you're doing something you have no business doing, hanging around rooms you haven't earned the right to be in, sitting at tables where everyone else seems to belong.

Here's the uncomfortable part. If you feel like your skill isn't good enough right now, it probably isn't. But that's not the insult it sounds like, that's information. 

You know what you don't know. That's the first useful thing you've got to work with.

Think about a plumber who's fixed a thousand toilets. He walks through your door with a kind of calm in his hands. He's seen this before. 

He'll see it again. But the first toilet, the very first job, would have been a curious, slightly terrifying task. 

The second a little less terrifying. The third, the fourth, the fifth, the twentieth. Each one a small deposit into something.

So how many lines of code do you need to write before you genuinely believe you're a programmer? A hundred thousand? More? 

How many words, articles, books, or scrawled drafts do you need to finally sit down at a desk and say, quietly, to yourself, that you're a writer? 

These aren't rhetorical questions. They just don't have clean answers.

Van Gogh died thinking he wasn't a painter. He painted almost every day. Thousands of canvases, thousands of hours, most of them spent not believing. 

That sounds absurd until you sit with it, and then it sounds completely familiar.

Here's another absurdity worth sitting with. Ask a drug addict if they're addicted. 

Most will say no. They snort, they smoke, they drink, every single day, reliably, without fail. The behaviour is there in full colour. But the attitude? Nowhere close. I'm not addicted.

The doing and the believing have split apart from each other, and somehow the belief wins the argument every morning.

That's the paradox worth naming. You can be doing the thing, fully, completely, every day, while your mind sits in the corner telling you you're not. 

Start chanting ohmmmmm over and over, and you might actually find yourself starting to relax. The behaviour came first. The feeling took a while to figure out it was invited.

There's a robot made by DeepMind called RoboCat. Before it can reliably reproduce any action, it needs somewhere between a hundred and a thousand demonstrations. 

The first few times it performs poorly, awkward, uncertain, failing in obvious ways. But the more it does it, the better it gets. The better it gets, the faster it picks up new tasks. With practice comes performance. The robot doesn't ask whether it's a robot yet. It just does the demonstrations.

So if you want to think a certain way, start behaving that way first. The belief won't arrive before the action, it doesn't work like that. If you want to think of yourself as a writer, write. If you want to think of yourself as an engineer, tinker, build, break things, and put them back together wrong and figure out why. 

If you want to think of yourself as a chef, cook, badly at first, then less badly, then one day something you'd actually serve to someone you like.

Change your actions and let your beliefs do the chasing. The behaviour is the real currency. The belief is just the receipt.

But here's the honest version: maybe they never catch up. Maybe like Van Gogh, you'll do the thing every day until the day you don't, and the belief will still be lagging somewhere behind. 

Trying to close that gap is exhausting work, you're fighting yourself instead of making something. Maybe that's why Van Gogh eventually took a knife to the side of his head. Though you have to wonder which came first: going crazy before you cut off your own ear, or cutting off your own ear and then going crazy.

Either way, if that's where it ends up, the question becomes what matters more. 

The thing or the thought of the thing. Do you want to think of yourself as a painter? Or do you want to paint? 

Those are very different ambitions, and only one of them produces paintings.

That's probably why Van Gogh exhibits exist in cities all over the world. He never thought of himself as a painter. He just painted. The paintings didn't care what he believed about himself, and neither did the people who came after.

I used to want to think of myself as a writer. Now I just write. I used to want to think of myself as an engineer. Now I just code. 

Maybe the attitude will catch up eventually. Maybe it won't. Either way, trying to force the two together is exhausting in the way that only self-imposed friction can be.

The other side of the coin is worse though. 

Attitude without behaviour. Thinking you're something without ever doing the thing. No skin in the game, no calluses, no real hours in. 

That version should worry you far more than imposter syndrome ever could.

And sometimes knowing less works in your favour. There's a kind of beginner's luck that isn't really luck at all, it's the attitude of someone who doesn't yet know what they're supposed to be afraid of, so they just go ahead and do it. They show up with an open mind and enough nerve to act on it. When you're starting from there, time becomes an ally instead of an opponent.

That's a wedding I'd like to go to. Beginner's mind and skill, getting to know each other slowly over years of work together. The wise old man who still looks at the world like it's his first morning in it.

Be grateful for the people who try to do the thing before they think they can. Before they've earned the credentials, before the belief has shown up, before anyone's given them permission. 

There's nothing more energising than watching someone create, try, make something, give it a real go, in spite of the uncertainty, in spite of the voice that says who do you think you are.

That's my favourite kind of attitude. Not the belief that you can do the thing. Just the action, quietly, while the belief is still somewhere on its way.

**23. Quality vs Quantity**

I refresh the feed.

Nothing new. Refresh again.

Still nothing. Refresh.

Somewhere between the second and third refresh, I've already forgotten why I opened my phone.

More is a hunger that doesn't know it's full.

Seven cups?

Hands shaking, heart racing, staring at the ceiling at 2am wondering where it all went wrong.

Same substance. Wildly different experience.

More didn't make it better. More made it worse.

Here's a strange one.

A puddle after rain.

Look into it. You'll see the sky, clouds drifting, maybe a bird,
the whole theatre of it.

Now look up at the actual sky.

More sky, yes. Much more.

Quality is the stubborn thing.

The cup of tea gone cold on your desk.

The ten-minute walk you took and barely noticed.

The conversation that felt ordinary but you still think about.

None of it was more.

All of it was enough.

**24. Life Is All About Taking Risks**

A writer posted something wrong on the internet last week. He knew it was probably wrong. He posted it anyway. 

Two things happened: someone told him he was right, and someone else told him exactly where he was wrong. He came out ahead either way.

That's the game. Life rewards the ones who take on risk but set the table so they always win. Not gambling, something smarter. A small downside on one side, everything on the other.

People relate to scars. Not capes. Superman is invincible, untouchable, barely human. 

Clark Kent trips on the pavement and apologizes twice. That's the one you root for. That's the one you see yourself in. The scars are what make you worth listening to.

There's a law for this. Cunningham's Law: if you want the right answer on the internet, don't ask a question, post the wrong answer. Post the thing. Let it be imperfect. 

Let people push back. They either gain something from it, their win, or they tell you what's missing, your win. You walk away better either way. The downside is small. Someone says you're wrong. 

The upside is open-ended. You learn, you grow, someone finds you because of what you made.

That's a system I want to be inside of. One with no cap. A job has a cap. There is always someone above you, and someone above them, and someone above them too. 

You work harder and the ceiling stays where it is. You play inside someone else's system and you are playing their game, not yours.

Working for yourself removes the ceiling. You decide the effort. You decide the direction. 

You can look at where society is going and move toward it, not against it, not beside it, toward it, with your arms out. There is a man who rows his boat every morning. Arms burning, back bent, fighting the water all the way across. 

And there is a man who puts up a sail. Both cross the lake. One uses effort. The other uses something that was already there. The internet is that sail. It was always blowing.

And if you fail? You get the scars. Something real. Something that makes you Clark Kent instead of Superman. 

You get the story that someone else needs to hear, the version of yourself that people can actually root for. Failure is not the opposite of winning inside a good system. 

It is part of how you win.

Post the thing. Take the risk. Put up the sail.

**25. Why So Serious?**

I was at the cinema when the Joker came out. Packed house. A Tuesday night.

People were dead quiet the whole way through.

Not the good kind of quiet. The kind where everyone's holding something in.

The film became the highest-grossing R-rated movie made.

I have an idea why.

It wasn't the violence.

It wasn't the performance, even though it was something.

It was the recognition.

Kids stealing from him. Boss firing him. Caring for his ageing mother in a tiny apartment. Standing in front of his idol and getting nothing back.

The ordinary accumulation of being ignored.

We've all felt it.

The pain of being left in the dark.

And that pain either consumes you or it makes you.

The most powerful scene is when he comes dancing down those stairs.

Gary Glitter's Rock and Roll thumping out.

He's not running from anything anymore.

He's given in.

Become himself.

There's something terrifying about it.

And something deeply relieving.

That's how you can spot a really swinging human being.

Not the loudest person in the room.

Not the most successful one, whatever that means.

The one who's stopped listening to the voices telling them to be one way or another. And started moving to their own thing.

The ultimate test isn't avoiding the darkness. It's learning to dance with it.

Most people spend their whole lives trying to outrun it. Staying busy. Keeping the calendar full. Filling the silence with noise.

The darkness catches up eventually.

Always does.

Better to learn the steps.

And look.

If you're not dancing through life, what are you doing here?

**26. Three Years Out**

Stand far enough from a painting and it blurs.

Step closer and it sharpens. A face. A hand. A door left open.

I can't see thirty years ahead. Nobody can. And I've stopped trying.

Thirty years is too far. My 54 year old self will live in a world I can't picture right now. Different jobs, different tools, different everything. Trying to plan for him feels like leaving a note for a stranger.

Three years I can see.

Not clearly. But close enough.

So I've been writing down what I want to be true in the next three years. Not goals with deadlines. Not a roadmap. Just things I want to reach out and hold.

I want to be genuinely good at this, AI, machine learning, building things with software. Not functional. Actually good.

I want my parents to be healthy. My mother, my father. That matters more than most things I think about. My sister too, finding her footing in work and life.

Four workouts a week. Because the person flat on a hospital bed wishes for one thing only. Health is the door everything else walks through.

I want to work on my stammer. Not fix it, that's the wrong word. Work with it. Become someone who can say what he means and be heard clearly.

Write more. This. Exactly this.

Start something of my own. I don't know what yet. But I want to stop waiting to find out.

Own a real estate, and make it a home with someone special.

That's three years.

After that, I can't see.

But step close enough and anything sharpens.
